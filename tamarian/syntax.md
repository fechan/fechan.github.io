## Sentences
### Metatopics

Tamarian is a [topic-prominent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topic-prominent_language) language, and most sentences are composed of the topic of the metaphor (the **metatopic**), and a comment about the topic (the **metacomment**).

In the following example, the metatopic is Darmok, marked with the second person topic (<small-caps>TOP.2</small-caps>) clitic, **=a**. The rest of the sentence comments on Darmok's location in the metaphor:

<div class="gloss">
  Darmoka talaki.<br/>
  Darmok=a tala-ki<br/>
  Darmok=*TOP.2* tala-*SUPE* <br/>
  ! '#Darmok (you) on the ocean.' <br/>
  ! 'You are lonely.' 
</div>

Notice the two different translations: the "literal" translation ('#Darmok (you) on the ocean'), and the true translation ('You are lonely'). The idea here is that, within the metaphor, Darmok is lonely. When adding the second person topic clitic **=a**, you assert that the second person ('you') is like Darmok, but in the real world.

In general, the sentence's true topic (in this case, 'you') is represented by metaphor's topic ('Darmok'). Whatever role or state the metatopic takes on in the metaphor, the true topic is understood to have that same role or state in the real world.

<details class="conlanger-note" markdown="1">
  <summary>Conlanger note: Topic marking the "wrong" thing</summary>

  Normally, metatopics are people, like Darmok, Jalad, Mirab, and Uzani. But what if you have the same metaphor (e.g. Darmok on the ocean), and you put the topic marker on the ocean instead, like `ocean=TOP.2 Darmok-SUBJ.1`? The ocean becomes the focus, and maybe it means "you make me feel lonely/you dump me?"

  What's the difference between that and `Darmok-TOP.1 ocean=SUPE.2`? Just focus? Maybe it has similar semantics to the English passive voice: "I was dumped by you".
</details>

#### Plural metasubjects
Metatopics are NPs, which can contain conjunctions:

<div class="gloss">
  Darmok e Jalads Tanagraro.<br/>
  Darmok e Jalad=s Tanagra-ro<br/>
  Darmok and Jalad=*TOP.1* Tanagra-*INE* <br/>
  ! '#Darmok and Jalad (us) at Tanagra.' <br/>
  ! 'We cooperate (with each other).'
</div>

When the metatopic contains more than one noun, the action or state that it represents must be reciprocal.

### Metacomments

A metatopic, like Darmok, might appear in a number of different metaphors. Darmok might be lonely in one metaphor but content in another, so the rest of the sentence (the metacomment) is necessary to know which metaphor is being used.

In the following sentence, we change the metacomment from **talaki** *'on the ocean'* to **bagaro** *'in the swamp'*. Notice how the meaning of the sentence changes:

<div class="gloss">
  Darmoka bagaro.<br/>
  Darmok=a baga-ro<br/>
  Darmok=*TOP.2* swamp-*INE* <br/>
  ! '#Darmok (you) in the swamp.' <br/>
  ! 'You are going insane from lack of socializing.'
</div>

In both sentences (1) and (2), the true topic is represented by the same metatopic, Darmok. However, since the metacomment is different, the state of being that Darmok is in changes from loneliness to insanity.

### Subjects

Within metacomments, there can be subjects, marked with the subject (<small-caps>SUBJ</small-caps>) suffix *-el*. Semantically, they are attributed to the metatopic in some way, and if there is a verb, the verb applies to the subject instead of the metatopic.

<div class="gloss">
  Mirabs rengh-el bati.<br/>
  Mirab=s rengh-el bati<br/>
  Mirab=*TOP.1* sail-*SUBJ* open<br/>
  ! '#Mirab (me), with sails unfurled.' <br/>
  ! 'I depart.'
</div>

<details class="conlanger-note" markdown="1">
  <summary>Conlanger note: Person marking for subjects</summary>

  I wonder if I should have person marking for subjects in the same way that metatopics are person-marked.

  There are some cases where it makes no sense to mark person, like if the subject is a body part: in "Gramble, throat slit", it makes no sense for "throat" to represent anything but a part of the topic. Maybe subjects that are *inalienable* from the metatopic should agree with the metatopic in person, or perhaps use a generic subject marker.

  For alienable ones, it might make some sense: in "Uzani, army with fists open", "army" could be associated a 3rd person (like "peanut butter"), for example, to mean "I spread the peanut butter thin", or something.
</details>
