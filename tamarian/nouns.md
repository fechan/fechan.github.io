## Simple nouns
**Simple nouns**, or nouns that are not compounds and not names, are a closed class in Tamarian.

* **tala** *'ocean, lake, large static body of water'*
* **baga** *'swamp, bog, dirty place'*
* **rengh** *'sail, wheel, engine, driving force'*

## Names
When naming people or places, a Tamarian is expected to create an entirely new name rather than reuse an existing name. This stems from a belief that using anothers' as someone is equivalent to identity theft, or stolen valor. Similarly, reusing a toponym insults the memory of the people who lived there.

If a person or place was originally known by a non-Tamarian name, such as Picard, this name is preferred, instead of making up a new name.

<details class="conlanger-note" markdown="1">
  <summary>Conlanger note: Why?</summary>

  I made up this aspect of Tamarian culture because in the Tamarian language, it's helpful to have a large amount of different names, since each name will get loosely associated with a semantic domain. (See (Tamarian Semantics)[/tamarian/semantics#archetypes] for more details.)
  
  Since new metaphors are created when a new situation occurs, and since they all tend to contain the name of a person involved, then if everyone named their child after the mythical warrior Uzani, the name *Uzani* will become associated with all of those metaphors. If some guy who happened to be named *Uzani* turns out to be a lifelong pacifist all their life becomes a part of lots of metaphors, suddenly the semantics of *Uzani* becomes confused: if you hear it in a metaphor, it would be harder to know if it was war-related or peace-related.

  That's not necessarily a problem since the origins of each metaphor aren't learned by most speakers anyway, so learning metaphors is just like learning any other vocabulary item. But I think having a sense of the general semantic category that a metaphor is in based on the names involved is cool.
</details>
