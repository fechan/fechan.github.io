## Tamarian in Star Trek
The original [Tamarian language](https://memory-alpha.fandom.com/wiki/Tamarian_language) was originally introduced in an episode of [*Star Trek: The Next Generation*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Trek:_The_Next_Generation) entitled ["Darmok"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darmok). In this episode, the crew of the starship Enterprise encounter an alien species whose language they cannot seem to translate with their [Universal Translator](https://memory-alpha.fandom.com/wiki/Universal_translator). Instead of the usual greetings like *"Hello"* or *"Leave our sovereign space at once!"*, all the translator will output is nonsense like **Darmok and Jalad at Tanagra** and **Shaka, when the walls fell**.

What they eventually come to realize is that all the expressions they're hearing are idioms from the Tamarian history and mythos that represent the speaker's intended meaning. For instance, **Darmok and Jalad at Tanagra** means *'cooperation'*.

My Tamarian conlang is an exploration of the ideas introduced in Star Trek. Since there is almost no description of the canon language outside of a few translated phrases in *The Next Generation* and the much later [*Lower Decks*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Trek:_Lower_Decks), we have a lot of room to work with when trying to turn Tamarian into an actual, usable language.

## A language of mostly dead metaphor
A common criticism of an idiom-based language is that in order for someone to learn such a language, all the idioms must be explained to the learner, and that these explanations must be delivered in a non-idiom-based language. If the non-idiom language were expressive enough to deliver the explanation, then there is no reason to use the idiom-based language.

However, this relies on a flawed assumption that idioms must be explained to be understood. In reality, we often learn the meaning of idioms in the same way we learn most of our vocabulary: by simply hearing its usage in context from someone else. This requires no explanation of its origins.

As an example, many Americans are familiar with the idiom **balls to the wall**, which is an adverb meaning *'with maximum effort or commitment'*. Very few people who use this idiom actually know that it references the ball-shaped grips on an aircraft's engine controls, and pushing the "balls to the wall" engages maximum thrust. Despite not knowing, this phrase is used all the time, and its idiomatic meaning is still commonly understood.

These idioms whose origins are completely opaque to most, if not all, of its users, are known as **dead metaphors**. They are learned like normal words too: there is nothing about the combination of the sounds in the word **bean** that let you deduce that it refers to a kind of food, nor did anyone need the etymology of the word explained to them to understand it.

With Tamarian, I turn these metaphors up to eleven: what if nearly the whole language is made of dead metaphors? Such metaphors act simply as vocabulary items, learned through context without etymology.

## Non-idioms
As presented in Star Trek, the Tamarian language can be literally translated into English without reflecting the intended meaning. At the end of the episode "Darmok", a new idiom is even created: **Picard and Dathon at El-Adrel**, meaning *'first contact'*. This implies there are words (or morphemes) that have literal meaning, such as names and whatever the language's equivalent of conjunctions and prepositions are.

In spite of this, most of the communication is still idiom-based. For my Tamarian conlang, I make the assumption that the morphemes that have literal meaning are very limited. They can express extremely basic spatial relations and objects, but nothing as complex or abstract as *'happiness'* or *'danger'*.

I also make the assumption that the literal morphemes are a **closed class**, like prepositions in English. While English nouns and verbs are an **open class**, meaning people can always make up more nouns and verbs, it is incredibly rare for English to gain a new preposition that catches on. If literal morphemes in Tamarian are a closed class, but idioms are an open class, then naturally the idioms are going to be more prevalent.

### The Universal Translator

To make the Tamarian language more practical to use, I will be adding a number of literal morphemes, such as pronominals and classifiers. This raises the question: if these *additional* morphemes exist, why aren't they additionally translated by the Universal Translator?

While the Universal Translator is capable of translating languages that humanity has never encountered, it is still unable to translate all the nuances of the language it's translating into English. In the [*Deep Space Nine*](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_Trek:_Deep_Space_Nine) episode ["Statistical Probabilities"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_Probabilities), the characters Jack, Lauren, and Patrick learn to speak Dominionese. By listening to the speech of a Dominion ambassador in his native language, the gang determine that while the Universal Translator translated one of his sentences as a declarative statement, the use of a particular grammatical construction (the *"passive voice transitive"*) actually indicates that it is a request.

Given that the Universal Translator has canonical shortcomings at translating grammatical nuances, I justify the inclusion of literal morphemes by making sure that the literal morphemes I add are all **functional morphemes**, meaning they exist to convey grammatical information, as opposed to lexical information like nouns and verbs. This way, all the additional morphemes I add will not get translated by the Universal Translator.
