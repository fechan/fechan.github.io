Sign constituents

Each sign may be specified for one or more of the following parameters, which together make up the sign. If a sign is not specified for a certain parameter, changes in that parameter does not alter its lexical meaning (though it may have grammatical significance).

Handshape
Configuration of the fingers, e.g. fist, flat hand, thumbs-up, etc.
Hand orientation
Direction the fingers and palm are pointing. When not specified, any orientation is allowed.
Hand constellation
Whether the hands are near, touching, clasped, etc. Never specified in 1-handed signs. If not specified in a 2-handed sign, the hands are apart by default.
Hand symmetry
In 2-handed signs, whether the hands mirror each other. Never specified in 1-handed signs. If not specified in a 2-handed sign, the hands are asymmetric and their location and handshape must be specified.
Location
Location where the sign is articulated.
Movement
The sign's movement.
Non-manuals
Articulations that do not involve the hand. If not specified, the facial expression and body posture is neutral by default.
Conlanger note: Choosing constituents

Since I like signs that can have all sorts of interesting configurations, and partly for convenience, I decided to include all of the ones you can notate in HamNoSys. Your conlang may not need to have all these constituents.

In scholarly documentation of sign languages worldwide, all of them discuss handshape as a parameter, and nearly all (>=94.25%) discuss movement and location. Other parameters, such as palm orientation, non-manuals, and number of hands are either not discussed in a significant number (>=41.37%) of the documentation, or explicitly say that it was looked for and not found to be phonemic in the language (>=6.89%). (Tatman 2015)

Conlanger note: Choosing handshapes

The handshapes I chose are the common set of 22 handshapes found in four different, unrelated sign languages in Rozelle 2003, plus a few that I wanted for certain signs such as hamfinger23,hamthumbacrossmod,hammiddlefinger,hamfingernail,hamindexfinger (crossed fingers) that I thought were cool. However, the sign languages analyzed in Rozelle 2003 each had from 34-49 handshapes total.

Due to the iconicity of sign languages, I occasionally found myself adding more handshapes to the chart the more signs I made, which would be like adding a phoneme to your phoneme chart just because you think a word would sound cool in your spoken conlang. I don't consider this a problem though, since Rozelle 2003 found that a language's most used handshapes are used with significantly greater frequency than the rest, with an exponential dropoff in usage as you go down in rank. However, I do try to always use an existing handshape in the inventory if I feel like it wouldn't destroy the "essence" of the sign I'm making.

Location inventory

The following locations may appear in signs. The hamlrat black square indicates the left or right side of a body part; for example, hamlrat,hamhead indicates left of head and hamhead,hamlrat indicates right of head. The hamlrbeside white square indicates a location next to the left or right of a body part.

    Head and face

  • hamhead Head
  • hamforehead, hamforehead,hamlrbeside Forehead
  • hameyes Eyes
  • hamear Ear
  • hamnose Nose
  • hamlrat,hamlips hamlips, hamlips,hamlrat Lips
  • hamchin Chin
  • hamunderchin Below chin

    Body and other

  • hamneutralspace Neutral space
  • hamneck, hamneck,hamlrat Neck
  • hamlrat,hamshoulders Shoulders
  • hamupperarm Upper arm
  • hamlowerarm Lower arm
  • hamwristback Back of wrist
  • hamlrat,hamchest hamchest Chest
  • hamstomach Stomach
Conlanger note: Choosing locations

I started with the common set of 24 locations found in four different, unrelated sign languages in Rozelle 2003. Like handshape, the usage frequency of a given location also drops exponentially with its rank. There was also a tendency for languages with larger handshape inventories to also have larger location inventories.

Conlanger note: Inventories for other parameters

I haven't found inventories of other parameters (like palm orientation or movement) for natural sign languages. For movement, it may simply be that there's just too many possibilities even in a single language to easily enumerate and, if that is the case, then it's not particularly useful to have such an inventory for a conlang either.

If you're trying to make a naturalistic sign language, it might be worth it to do additional research on variations in movement and other parameters across different sign languages.
